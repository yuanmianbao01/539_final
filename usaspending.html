Foundational Research to Improve Federal Spending Transparency

Length

Sept. 2022 - May 2023

Tools

Zoom, Miro, Excel

My Role

UX Researcher

Challenge

Who are the users?

As the official source of government spending data, USAspending is supposed to promote federal spending transparency by reaching as various types of US citizens as possible. However, the current use cases are mostly limited to specific groups of specialists, such as government employees and academic researchers.

To better promote federal spending transparency, I conducted a foundational research to understand who are the missed user groups and their needs. The research insights helped the design team to identify key design concepts for different user groups and to establish a prioritized roadmap.

Protocol

In-depth Interview

The research is designed to identify user groups and understand why USAspending.gov may not be effectively serving several groups. I planned a series of interviews using a semi-structured approach, which included in-depth interviews and usability testing sessions.

I first asked a set of questions to understand motivations and reasons why users find federal spending data relevant and of interest. Then I directed the participants to use USAspending.gov to look for the information of interest to assess how USAspending.gov performed in meeting users' needs and expectations.

Analysis

Generating Personas

In the analysis process, I paid specific attention to the goals to contextualize participants’ pain points and attitudes toward the website. Based on the similarity of goals across participants, I formalized four personas.

  • The Learner wants to satisfy personal curiosity about federal spending.
  • The Researcher wants to produce written reports using information on USAspending.gov.
  • The Opportunity Seeker wants to help themselves or others obtain federal award opportunities.
  • The Agency Fact Checker wants to comply with government reporting mandates.

Insights

The Interdisciplinary Nature of Users

The goal of a user on USAspneding.gov varies as the context varies, instead of being set in stone. For example, professors use the website as academic researchers for their own research projects, while they become learners when they facilitate students to learn about federal spending data in introductory classes.

With the interdisciplinary nature of users in mind, I helped the design team to shift their mindset from designing for occupations to designing for goals, which would promote the flexibility and inclusiveness of the website design.

Relatable-ness: Does the information matter to users?

We find that the group with the least use frequency are the learners. Why? That’s because they are unable to find the information that matters to them. Learners are not the president: they care more about spendings relatable to them instead of data of the whole federal government across different departments. What the learners look for are usually spending data that geographically related to their daily life or to their personal connections.

As we identified the learners as the biggest missed opportunity for the website, I assisted the design team to prioritize re-designing for learners to help them find their relatable information more efficiently and with less barriers.
